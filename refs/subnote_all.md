# Ch.1 SLA Theory

### SLA Theory


## Behaviorism


### 1) Structuralism


the view of language which states that a language can be dismantled into small pieces or units, and these pieces/units can be added up again to form the whole.

### 2) Audiolingual method


A method that emphasizes lg acquisition through continuous decontextualized drills to form lg habits.

- **Pattern drills**: a rote, intensive, controlled practice where there is a repetition of sounds by the Ss without the vaguest understanding of what the sound might possibly mean

- **Rote learning:** a learning type which involves the mental storage of isolated bits and pieces of items having little or no association with existing cognitive structure.
<-> **Meaningful learning (****Subsumption****) **

## Universal Grammar Model


Grammar of a lg is consisted of principles and parameters. FLA involves parameter setting, whereas SLA involves parameter resetting.

## Krashen's Input Hypothesis


### 1) Acquisition/ Learning Hypothesis


- **Acquisition**: an unconscious process that involves the naturalistic development of lg within natural communication context, with sufficient comprehensible input, where the focus is on meaning.

- **Learning:** a conscious process to get explicit knowledge about the formal properties of a TL

- **Non-interface position**: acquired knowledge and learnt knowledge are separated and unrelated.

### 2) Input Hypothesis


- Exposure to comprehensible input is the necessary and sufficient cause of SLA

- **Requirement of optimum input**: comprehensible, interesting/ relevant, not grammatically sequenced, sufficient amount, delivered in less defensive environment

- **Silent period**: Ss should never be required to produce speech in TL unless they are ready.

### 3) Affective Filter Hypothesis


- Motivation, confidence, anxiety affect affective filter

- **Affective filter**: a mental block which prevents L from fully utilizing the comprehensible input by filtering it out.

### 4) Natural Order Hypothesis


- The acquisition of grammatical structures in SL follows a predictable natural order

- **Zero option for grammar instruction**

### 5) Monitor Hypothesis


- Conscious learning only functions as a monitor or editor that checks and repairs the output initiated by acquired knowledge.

### 6) Criticism1: Swain's Output Hypothesis


- Output requires syntactic processing while input only requires semantic processing

- 3 Functions of output:
**a) ****noticing or triggering function:** realizing the gap between what they can say and what they want to say.
**b) ****hypothesis**** testing function**: pushed output makes L try out new rules and modify them in accordance to the reactions.
**c) ****metalinguistic**** ****funciton**: Pushed output helps a learner to actively reflect on language produced by the self or others and what they know about the target language system.

- Pushed output encourages Ls to formulate syntactically slightly more advanced structure

### 7) Criticism2: Schmidt's Noticing Hypothesis


- **Noticing hypothesis**: A hypothesis that claims that noticing may be an essential prerequisite for input to be converted into intake, and eventually integrated into Ls' interlanguage system.

- **Noticing the gap**: The process of L's cognitively comparing his own interlanguage and TL. This is important because it raises awareness of the L's incomplete interlanguage and induces better language learning. Corrective feedback (recast) and Dictogloss encourage Ss to notice the gap.

### 8) Criticism3: Outperformance of High Input Generators


- **LIG**: Ls who generate low levels of input by being passive.

- **HIG**: Ls who generate high levels of input by initiating and sustaining their turns. They convert more input to intake as they try out more hypotheses.

## Information Processing Theories


### 1) McLaughlin's Information Processing Model


- **Controlled processing:** A type of information processing which occurs when learner access info that is new, unfamiliar, or complex.

- **Automatic processing**: A type of information processing which occurs when L process a piece of info rapidly and effortlessly whenever the situation requires it.

- **Restructuring**: A process where L adjusts his existing knowledge to accommodate and internalize new info in the long-term memory.

- For enhancement in automaticity, L should use the lg in authentic context for meaningful purpose to overcome propensity to pay too much attention to bits of lg and process lg forms quickly to the periphery.

### 2) Byalystok's Implicit and Explicit Model


- **Explicit knowledge**: knowledge about lg and the ability to articulate this knowledge

- **Implicit knowledge**: information that is automatically and spontaneously used in lg tasks.

- **Interface position**: explicit knowledge can turn into implicit knowledge through practice (proceduralization = automatization)

- Implicit knowledge can be acquired through a) unconscious acquisition in natural communication context, or b) proceduralization of explicit knowledge by practice

### 3) Skill-acquisition Theory


- **Declarative knowledge**: knowledge that can be stated or declared
**Procedural knowledge:** an ability to use the knowledge

- Declarative knowledge can be proceduralized through practice

- Proceduralized knowledge reduces the load on Ls' working memory.

## Processability Model


### 1) Processability Model


- **Developmental readiness**: L2 structure can be acquired only if the L is developmentally ready to acquire it.

- **Bottom-up approach**: Linguistic features are learned from bottom-up sequence, smaller and simpler elements to longer and complex ones.

- Some sentences are formed by moving elements; therefore, movement is the key to understand the learning sequence.

### 2) Teachability Hypothesis


- This hypothesis states that language structure can be learnt from the formal instruction only if Ls' interlanguage approximates the point when this target structure is acquired in the natural setting.

- Comparison with Krashen's Natural Order hypothesis: Krashen asserts zero option for grammar whereas this hypothesis claims the need of formal instructions that are compatible with Ss' developmental status.

## Connectionism


### 1) Usage-based theory


- This theory assumes that lg learning is input-driven and a result of massive exposure to the language.

- Acquisition of lg is due to the development of strong association between items that are frequently encountered together at the same time.

## Competition Model


L comes to understand how to use cues where lg signals specific functions. Ex) Word order cues for agent

## Interaction Hypothesis


### 1) Argument


- **Original version**: Long asserted that comprehensible input is necessary but for this to be given, interactional modification must be preceded.

- **Updated version**: Long later asserted the importance of comprehensible output as this either provides Ls with corrective feedback or an opportunity to produce one's own output.

### 2) Functions of meaning negotiation


- Meaning negotiation makes input comprehensible through the decomposition and segmenting of input
Ex) L modifies one's output by segmenting message data: buvdaplate-> above-> above the plate

- Meaning negotiation provides corrective feedback

- Meaning negotiation pushes L to modify his output

### 2) Meaning Negotiation Sequence 


- **Trigger:** An utterance that causes communication breakdown to induce meaning negotiation

- Resolution: Indicator-> Response-> Reaction

- **Trigger:** She's a loner
**Signal:** Sorry?
**Response**: She stays away from others
**Follow-up**: Yeah, but I saw her at a party yesterday.

### 3) Means of modified interaction


- **Input modification**: lexical modification, syntactic modification, modification of pronunciation, nonverbal signals

- **Conversational modification**: confirmation check, comprehension check, clarification request, recast, self-repetition, backtracking

- Comprehension check is the speaker checking someone else's understanding (i.e.Do you understand, Are you with me?), while in the confirmation check is the speaker checking his/her understanding (i.e. A: I was really chuffed. B: You were pleased? OR You mean...?)

### 4) Counterevidence against acquisition via meaning negotiation


- Not all pushed output is modified output

- Absence of pushed output but simple acknowledgement

## Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory


### 1) Zone of Proximal Development


- **ZPD**: The distance between the actual developmental level(independent problem solving) and the potential developmental level(problem solving under adult guidance or more capable peers).

### 2) Imitation and Internalization


- When interacting with others, L intentionally imitate L2 features that they do not understand yet. This act of imitation leads Ls' 'internalization' of input in a learner's interlanguage system.

- While imitation in Behaviorism is non-creative, mechanical mimicry, imitation in sociocultural theory is a creative and transformative activity.

### 3) Scaffolding


- There are two definitions in scaffolding.
1) Supportive environment created through guidance or feedback that Ls receive during collaboration with more capable ones.
2) Interactional supports which enables L to perform or master what one has not been able to do independently.

- **Verbal guidance:** Supportive behaviors offered by more capable Ls or T to enable L to complete the task that is currently beyond his independent means.

- **Peer**** scaffolding:** Scaffolding among peers enabling them to perform and complete a task which would be too difficult for any one of them to complete independently.

### 4) Regulation


- Learning is a process of moving from 'object-regulation' to 'other-regulation' to 'self-regulation'.

- **Object regulation**: Ls' behavior is controlled by objects in their environment.
Ex) referring to a dictionary

- **Other regulation**: L gained some control over object, but still needs guidance from others
Ex) asking for help

- **Self-regulation:** L becomes skilled and able to act independently and autonomously.
Ex) inferring by oneself

### ETC)


1) Modified output, Pushed output, Uptake

2) Ls modify their speech more when they confront clarification request rather than confirmation check or recast.

3) **Cooperative learning**: A type of learning where Ss work in pairs or groups, share information, and play as a team to achieve common goals, with dependent on the socially structured exchange of information between Ls.

**Collaborative learning**: A type of learning where a learner engages with more capable peers who provide assistance and guidance.

# Ch.2 Linguistic Aspects of SLA

### Linguistic Aspect of SLA


## Contrastive Analysis Hypothesis


### 1) Types of Transfer


- **Transfer**: Impact of previous knowledge on subsequent learning

- **Positive transfer**: Previously learnt items facilitate learning of new items.

- **Negative transfer**

**Interference**: A type of negative transfer where a previous item is incorrectly transferred to or incorrectly associated with an item to be learned

**Overgeneralization**: A type of intralingual transfer generalizing a particular rule or item

- **Avoidance**: A type of L1 transfer where a L avoids the suing a certain grammatical structure in the TL because his/her native lg does not have such structure

- **Overuse**: A type of L1 transfer where L overuses a certain expression in TL even if it sounds unnatural.

- L1 transfer is consisted of positive transfer, interference, avoidance, and overuse.

### 2) Contrastive Analysis Hypothesis


- **Contrastive Analysis**: Procedures for comparing and identifying similarities and differences between the linguistic systems of different languages.

- **Hierarchy of difficulty**

- Minimal pair practice: CAH suggests minimal pair practice, which is in two kinds: **mechanical minimal pairs**; **contextualized minimal pairs**.

- Weak version of CAH: Emphasizes cross-linguistic influence; stresses both facilitative/ debilitative effects

## Interlanguage


### 1) Characteristics of interlanguage


- **Interlangauge**: An intermediate system located somewhere between a L's native lg and his/her TL

- Systematic, Variable, Dynamic

### 2) Developmental stages of interlanguage


- **Presystematic**** stage**: Ls commit random errors; no consistency in Ls' production

- **Emergent stage**: Some consistency; Backsliding; No self-correction
**Backsliding**: L seems to grasp a particular rule but regress to some previous stage

- **Systematic stage**: More consistency; Ability to self-correct

- **Stabilization**: Few errors; Self-correction without being pointed out; fossilization may happen
**Fossilization**: A relative permanent incorporation of incorrect linguitic forms into a Ls' second language competence

## U-shaped learning and Restructuring


### 1) Restructuring


- **Restructuring**: A qualitative change caused by the integration of new elements into a Ls' interlanguage system in a way which triggers reorganization of the Ls' interlanguage system

### 2) U-shaped learning


- **U-shaped learning**: A phenomenon moving from a correct form to an incorrect form and then back to the correct form

- Regression is caused by overgeneralization

- Correct production in the beginning may be the result of imitation or Ls' perceiving it as a separate lexical item.

## Error Analysis


### 1) Types of Errors


- **Interlingual**** errors**: Errors caused by interlingual transfer,b interference
(e.g. 'see an exam', 'do a shower')

- **Intralin****gual**** errors**: Errors caused by intralingual transfer, such as overgeneralization
(e.g. 'I goed to the store')

- **Simplification****:** Error caused by omitting an obligatory grammatical marker
(e.g. 'two year)

- **Communication-based errors**: Errors caused by a Ls' use of communication strategies
(e.g. 'airball' for 'balloon)

- **Induced errors**: Errors that are caused by Ls' faulty hypotheses about the lg due to teachers' misleading explanation or faulty presentation of a word in textbooks.
(e.g. *she cries as if [like] a baby, 'point at'/ 'point out')

- **Global errors**: Errors that hinder communication
**Local errors**: Errors that does not prevent a message to be understood

- **Overt errors**: Errors that can be noticed on the sentence level
**Covert errors**: Errors that are grammatically well formed at the sentence level, but not interpretable within the context of communication.

### 2) Feedback


- **Feedback**: A response that L receives regarding the success or failure of his/her language production

- **Affective feedback**: Feedback that is encoded with kinetic mechanism such as gestures, tone of voices, facial expressions. It should always be positively delivered.

- **Cognitive feedback**: Feedback that is conveyed by linguistic devices.

**Positive feedback**: Feedback that confirms Ls' utterance regarding its correctness.
(e.g. Good job!)

**Negative feedback**: Corrective feedback in response to a Ls' erroneous utterances; it can either directly draw Ls' overt attention to the error (i.e. explicit feedback) or does not draw any overt attention to the error (i.e. implicit feedback)

- **Extensive feedback**: Feedback that is provided on a wide range of forms
**Intensive feedback**: Feedback that is provided on certain preselected forms

- Conversational feedback involves negotiation of meaning, whereas pedagogical feedback involves negotiation of forms.

### 3) Evidence


- **Evidence**: Information about whether a certain structure is permissible in the TL system

- **Positive evidence**: Information that confirms that certain utterances are permissible

- **Negative evidence**: Information that suggests certain utterances are not permissible

a) Pre-emptive vs. Reactive

b) Explicit vs. Implicit

### 8) Error corrective Feedback 


### Implicit feedback


A response to a Ls' error that does not draw overt attention to the error and is provided during communicative interactions; therefore, ungrammaticality is implied

### 1) Recast


- Reformulation of the whole or part of a Ls' erroneous utterance into a correct form while maintaining the overall focus on meaning without altering the central meaning.

- Contains both positive and negative evidence.

- Trigger notice the gap process

- Make Ss focus on form

- Generally implicit and unobtrusive, but its explicitness can vary.

- If recast is implicit, it can also be interpreted as a confirmation check

- If recast is explicit, it may draw a Ls' attention to correct form effectively. In this case, additional intonational signals are added.
(A: She catched the dog. B: Oh, she CAUGHT the dog.)

- Recast may not lead to successful uptake if the recast is given
a) when Ls are not developmentally ready,
b) or when Ls' attention to meaning blocks his recognition of forms.
(e.g. She holded the baby rabbits/ Did you say your mom held the baby rabbits? / Yes, she holded them tightly)

### 2) Clarification request


- An utterance which indicates to a learner that his/her utterance has not been understood or that the utterance is ill-formed in some way and that a repetition or a reformulation is required. (e.g. Pardon? What do you mean by ~?)

- L produce either linguistically more accurate utterance OR semantically transparent sentence.

- Always recognized as corrective feedback, and functions as a prompt (i.e. utterances in response to a Ls' error used to elicit her self-correction)

- Likely to elicit pushed/modified output; Produce opportunity to self-repair

### Explicit feedback 


A response to Ls' error that directly draws overt attention to the error in some way; the information about ungrammaticality is directly conveyed.

### 1) Repetition


- Teacher's reiteration of the Ls' erroneous utterance with exaggerated intonation to highlight the error
(e.g. A: I pay only 10 dollars. B: I pay? (with rising intonation))

- Provide opportunity to self-repair

### 2) Explicit correction


- The explicit provision of correct form in response to Ls' error after clearly pinpointing the error

- It identifies the error and then overtly corrects it.

- Does not provide opportunity to self-repair

### 3) Metalinguistic feedback


- Points out the nature of the Ls' error by using metalinguistic comment, information, or questions without explicitly providing the correct form; it refers to feedback that provides the L with metalinguistic comments in the form of a statement or a question about the correctness of an utterance.

- In case of lexical error, word definition can be given as metalinguistic information.

- **Metalinguistic**** comment**: Can you find the error? You see your mistake?

- **Metlinguistic**** questions:** What is the past tense of this verb?

- **Metalinguistic**** information**** or ****metalinguistic**** clue**: It is a past tense.

### 4) Elicitation


- A technique in response to L's error used to directly elicit the correct form from the L by incomplete sentences (e.g. He comes...), questions (e.g. How do you say X in French?), or request for reformulation (e.g. Can you say it again?).

- Overtly draws attention to the problematic forms

- Pushes L to self-correct

## Modified Input


### 1) Types of Modified input


- **Care-taker Talk**: The simple speech used by parents or babysitters when they talk to young children who are learning to talk; Usually modified by simplification, elaboration, slow pronunciation

- **Foreigner Talk:** Modified speech used by native speakers of a lg when they speak to foreigners who are not proficient in the lg.

Louder, exaggerated pronunciation, simple vocabulary or grammatical structures, topicalization or repetition

- **Teacher Talk**: Input provided by the T which S in classroom are exposed to.

### 2) Types of Modification of input


- **Simplification**: Lexical modification/ simplificaiton; Grammatical modification/ simplification

- **Elaboration****:** Restating ideas of the original input in different ways; Repeating segments of the input; Positive effect on SLA

- **Regularization**: Selecting forms that are more basic or explicit than original ones; Using explicit markers, the movement of topics, Using lexical items with a wide coverage.

### Learner Variables


## Learner Style


### 1) Perceptive preference

- Visual Style

### 2) Personality preference


- Extroverted Style
Introverted Style

- Tolerance of ambiguity: A willingness to deal with uncertainty or new stimuli within second language learning context; too high ambiguity tolerance may lead to fossilization or prevention of meaningful learning.
(e.g. High ambiguity tolerance: Even though Sujin doesn't understand everything, it does not bother her)

Intolerance of ambiguity
(e.g. Low ambiguity tolerance: Mijin doesn't like reading something in English because she feels that she should always look up unfamiliar words in a dictionary)

### 3) Processing preference


- **Field Dependence**: More holistic in their perceptions and are less able to distinguish specific elements within a larger context.
**Field Independence**: More analytic and are able to ignore the larger visual field in order to find specific objects within it.

F.I. Ls perform better in selective listening as they can well distinguish parts from whole. They are also better at mechanical drills which do not require additional situational context.

- Left-brain dominance
Right-brain dominance

- Reflectivity
Impulsivity

If given preparation time before speaking task, reflective Ls may gain confidence, and impulsive Ls may gain accuracy.

- Global-oriented
Detail-oriented

- Synthetic style
Analytic style

- Deductive style
Inductive style

### 4) Learning Context preference


- **Concrete learning style:** A style in which an individual prefers direct means of processing information, is people-oriented, spontaneous, imaginative, emotional..

- **Analytic learning style**: prefer hypothetical-deductive reasoning; object-oriented; independent; prefer logical and didactic presentation.

- **Communicative learning style**: independent from Ts; adaptable and flexible; high ambiguity tolerance; prefer social learning

- **Authority-oriented learning style**: needs Ts' directions and explanations; prefer a structured learning environment; low tolerance of ambiguity; prefer a sequential progression.

**Style stretching skill**: A skill where Ss are able to respond flexibly in different learning context by using a new learning style they are not accustomed to

### Learning Strategies


### 1) Metacognitive Strategies


- **Metacognitive**** Strategies**: High-order strategies which plan the upcoming learning before the actual learning, monitor one's comprehension and production while learning, and evaluate the outcome of learning after learning.

- advance organizers; directed attention; selective attention; self-management; functional planning; self-monitoring; delayed production; self-evaluation.

- Self-report data(low validity), Self-observation, Self-revelation(Think aloud) data

- Writing a learning log means the S is employing metacognitive strategies

### 2) Cognitive Strategies


- **Cognitive Strategies**: Learning strategies that are more limited to specific learning tasks and are deployed to manipulate the learning material.

- repetition; resourcing; translation; imagery; auditory representation; grouping; note taking; deduction; inducing; keyword; transfer; elaboration; recombination; contextualization; inferencing

### 3) Socioaffective Strategies


- **Social ****Strategies**: Interpersonal behavior aimed at increasing the amount of L2 communication.
**Affective Strategies**: Taking control of one's emotional conditions and experiences that shape one's subjective involvement in learning.

- Cooperation, Question for clarification, Self-talk

- Lack of social strategies: 'I didn't ask her to repeat it because I wasn't sure if it was okay'

### Communication Strategies


Strategies that a lg L employs to compensate for his/her gaps in speaking competence, thereby solving possible communication problems in reaching particular communicative goal.

### 1) Avoidance Strategies


- **Topic avoidance**** **

- **Lexical avoidance**

- **Pronunciation avoidance**

- **Message abandonment**

### 2) Compensatory Strategies

Strategic options used by Ls to overcome their self-perceived weaknesses and to maintain communication flow with preventing communication breakdown.

- **Prefabricated pattern**: Stock chunks of language memorized by rote without internalized knowledge; usually for survival purpose
(e.g. Where is the toilet? I don't speak French. I don't understand you)

- **Appeal for assistance**: Seeking aids from the interlocutor either directly or indirectly
(e.g. What do you X in Spanish? Tell me what X is in English / eye contact, puzzled facial expression)

- **Code-switching**: Using L1 word with L1 pronunciation within a stream of speech in L2.
(e.g. When I didn't have enough words to describe it, I simply used Korean words)

- **Literal translation**: Literally translating a lexical item, compound, or structure. May result from L1 interference.
(e.g. 'pig meat' for 'pork', 'I have nothing to say' for 'I have no excuse', 'thick tea' for 'strong tea')

- **Foreignizing**: Using a L1 word by adjusting it to L2 phonology or L2 morphology
(e.g. You are so 유아tic, Can I borrow your yeon-pill?)

- **Approximation****:** Using an alternative term which shares enough semantic features in common with the target lexical item so expresses the meaning of it as closely as possible
(e.g. 'pipe' for 'waterpipe', 'squirrel' for 'chipmunk', 'worm' for 'silkworm', 'counting machine' for 'calculator')

- **Circumlocution****:** Describing the characteristics or elements of the target object or action instead of using an appropriate lexical item.
(e.g. 'the thing that make hair hot' for 'blow dryer', 'something you use when you want to clean things' for 'vacuum cleaner', 'the potatoes that you eat with burgers' for 'French fries', 'put chickens in oil, very hot oil, kind of bake them' for 'frying')

- **Word coinage**: Creating a non-existing L2 word based on a supposed rule
(e.g. 'vegentarianist' for 'vegetarian', 'cooker' for 'a person who cooks')

- **Use of all-****purpose words**: Extending a general, empty lexical items to context where specific words are lacking
(e.g. thing, stuff)

- **Stalling****:** Using fillers or hesitation devices to gain some time to think

- **Nonlinguistic signals**: Using mime, gesture, facial expression, or sound imitation

## Affective Aspects


### 1) Motivation


- **Integrative orientation**: Predisposition of Ss to learn the lg as they want to integrate in L2-using speech community
(e.g. English will allow one to converse and be with various international speakers of English)
**Instrumental orientation**: Predisposition of Ss to learn the lg for the practical reasons such as increased occupational opportunities
(e.g. English will add to her social status)

- **Intrinsic orientation****:** Predisposition of Ss to learn the lg for itself and for pleasure without expecting apparent external rewards. It rather brings internal rewards such as feeling of satisfaction and competence.
**Extrinsic orientation**: Predisposition of Ss to learn as they are expecting extrinsic rewards from outside and beyond the selves.
(e.g. Showing one's ability to his/her family and friends)

### 2) Inhibition and Language Ego


- **Language ego**: The identity that a person develops in reference to the language he or she speaks.

- **Inhibition**: a mental defensive mechanism which a language learner builds in order to protect his or her fragile egos.

- **Adaptive language ego****:** Second language identity with which a language learner is able to flexibly deal with identity crisis involved in L2 learning with permeable ego boundary.

- Lowering inhibition: guessing games; meaning-centered, fluency-based activities; teacher's empathy and patience; challenging but not overwhelming tasks.

### 3) Anxiety


- **Facilitative anxiety**: Helpful tension especially closely related to competitiveness in language learning context; it motivates a learner make efforts to overcome the sense of anxiety.
(e.g. High level of challenge makes her feel nervous but this tension pushes her to do well in them.)

- **Debilitative anxiety**: Feelings of worry that are perceived as detrimental to one's self-efficacy or that hinder one's performance; it causes a learner to avoid the source of anxiety.

### 4) Risk-taking


- **Risk-taking**: Willingness to use the TL system with an attitude to take the risk of making errors.
(e.g. Risk-taking behavior: Even though she feels nervous, she tried to speak whenever she can)

- Optimum level of risk-taking

### 5) Empathy


- **Empathy:** An ability to put oneself in the position of another person in order to understand him or her better.

### 6) Willingness to Communicate (WTC)


- **WTC**: The intention to initiate communication, given a choice.

(e.g. High WTC: One enjoys participating in small group speaking activities)

### <Sociocultural Factors and Culture Teaching) 


### 1) Acculturation Model


- Acculturation involves a) acquisition of a second identity, b) learning the culture of the L2 community, c) adapting to those values and behavioral patterns.

- **Preservation**: Ls who reject TL culture and cling to their native culture
**Acculturation**: Ls who view TL culture favorably, but also maintain native culture and lg.
**Assimilation**: Ls who acculturate into the TL culture at the expense of native culture and lg.

- **Social distance**: A distance determined by social relationships between the group of second language learners and the target language group

## Communicative Competence


### 1) Swain's Framework

- **Sociolinguitic**** Competence**: a) a sensitivity to dialect or variety, b) a sensitivity to register, c) an ability to interpret cultural references and figures of speech

## Exchange Structure in Classroom


### 1) IRE (Initiation-Response-Evaluation) Sequence


- **Display question**: Question that elicit language practice. This question has predetermined answer which is known to the teacher

- Merits: 1) Useful framework in controlled lg practice, 2) Beginners can predict the structure 3) Useful in error correction, awareness raising, comprehension check, scaffolding

- Demerits: 1) Ts take most initiatives, 2) No interaction 3) Ss become passive

### 2) IRF (Initiation-Response-Feedback) Sequence


- **Referential question**: Question that asks for real information that is not known to T.

- Merits: 1) Useful in communicative practice, fluency exercise, meaning-centered activity, 2) Promotes real, authentic communication

## Four modes of Classroom Discourse


### Managerial mode

A mode of classroom discourse where a teacher exclusively dominates turns without students' participation or turns for the purpose of conveying instructions or directions for upcoming learning.

### Material mode

A mode of classroom discourse where topic and turn-taking are determined by materials.

### Skills and systems mode

A type of classroom discourse goal of which is accurate utterance of students by getting them to manipulate the target language system with engagement in language practice.

### Classroom context mode

A type of classroom discourse which resembles natural and casual conversations outside of the class in that turn-taking and topic shift are managed by learners , with a teacher playing a role of equal partner, taking a seat back, and keeping error correction minimal.

## Cohesion and Coherence


### 1) Cohesion


- The grammatical and lexical relationships between utterances in a discourse or sentences in a text.

- **Grammatical cohesion**: Reference, Substitution, Ellipsis, Discourse connectors

- **Lexical cohesion**: Collocation, Synonym/Antonym, Superordinate, Repetition, General Things

### 2) Coherence


- Relationships which link the meanings of utterances in a discourse or of sentences in a text; coherence forms covert, logical connections among sentences or between sentences and our general world knowledge.

- A text may be coherent but incohesive, or cohesive but incoherent

### 3) Functional Coherence and Pragmatic Failure


- **Functional Coherence**: This is established when one speaker's utterance with a specific function is followed by the other speaker's utterance with another function which is congruent with the previous one.

- **Pragmatic failure**: Miscommunication caused because one person misinterprets the function of the other persons' utterance.

## Schema Theory

### Content schemata and Formal schemata

### 2) Schematic knowledge


- General world knowledge

- Sociocultural knowledge

- Genre knowledge

According to Schema Theory, general world knowledge, sociocultural knowledge, and topic knowledge are included in CONTENT SCHEMATA, and genre knowledge is included in FORMAL SCHEMATA.

## Input


### 1) Materials


- **Authentic Material****: **Written texts taken from real life and not specifically manipulated for the purpose of lg teaching and learning

- T selected two different reading texts from a magazine.

- **Simulated authentic materials****: **Teacher-created written texts which mimic the content, format and tone of the authentic materials with simpler lg.

- **Modified materials: **Adapted texts in which lg and cultural references have changed in order to provide Ss with comprehensible input.
This type of material benefit low-level readers as it builds self-confidence in them.

### 2) Criteria of selecting a reading material


- **Suitability of content: **A criterion for choosing reading texts that determines whether the texts contain interesting, enjoyable, challenging materials for Ss. ** **

- **Exploitability: **A criterion for choosing reading texts which determines whether the texts contain exploitable materials that match instructional goals.

- **Readability: **A criterion for choosing reading texts which determines whether the lexical and structural difficulty of the texts is appropriate for the Ss' level of proficiency

## Process of Reading


### 1) Bottom-up Processing


- **Bottom up Processing: **Decoding process in which meaning is decoded and built up from the smallest units of the given input to a complete text.

- **Lower-level skill**: A skill which allows a reader to rapidly and automatically recognize words in a text. (word recognition)

- Lower-level of reading processes; data-driven; text-based; focuses on lg itself

- Comprehension Qs require factual information that is stated in the text

- Phonics approach

- Intensive reading

- Learning is a gradual, linear and additive process.

- Decoding oral utterances, discriminating btwn individual sounds, in particular those with minimal contrasts, and identifying different stress, rhythm and intonation patterns feature in the early stages of learning.

### 2) Top-down Processing


- **Top down Processing: **A reconstruction process in which meaning is inferred and reconstructed from the application of a reader's prior knowledge

- **Higher-level skill**: A skill which allows a reader to comprehend and interpret the text. (e.g. identifying a main idea in a text)

- Use of background knowledge; making predictions; searching the text to confirm or reject the predictions

- Conceptually-driven; knowledge-based; Meaning-generating activities

- Predicting-> Sampling-> Confirming-> Correcting

- Comprehension Qs require inferencing process of Ss, which promote the use of background knowledge

- Whole Language Approach

- Extensive reading

- The difficulty can be minimized by letting them hear the text as often as necessary and providing a range of activities of increasing complexity.

### 3) Interactive Processing


- **Interactive Processing: **A processing where bottom-up and top-down processing are adopted simultaneously.

### 4) Interactive-compensatory Model


- **Interactive-compensatory Model: **Lg processing is organized into levels ranging from letter recognition to high-level schemata, and a deficiency of processing at one level can be compensated by reliance on processing at another level.

- Ss' deficiency in bottom-up skills can be compensated by her top-down skills.

- Processing at one level can compensate for deficiencies at another level

- Rs who lack fast automatic word recognition skills would try to compensate by using more controlled activation of processes at higher levels, like contextual information and top-level schemata.

## Types of Reading Process


### 1) Intensive Reading/ Extensive Reading


- **Intensive Reading: **Reading a short text and understanding it in depth and with the explicit purpose of leaning a new reading skills and/or learning new lg items. It is usually followed by exercises or activities to check reader's comprehension or his use of reading strategy.

- Promotes the mastery learning of each sublevel before learning the whole concept.

- Reading involves a passive and linear processing of information

- **Extensive Reading: **Reading longer texts which are selected by Ss themselves, for pleasure and for real-world purposes, without a focus on classroom exercises designed to test comprehension skills.

This elicits implicit learning and automatization of Ls' bottom-up reading skills.

This involves book-conferencing and sustained silent reading. (SSR)

Open library in the classroom and school library may be beneficial.

Ts may ask Ss to report on what they have read.

- Extensive reading increases Ss' passive vocabulary

### Teaching Listening


## Background knowledge in Listening


The lack of shared schematic information makes comprehension difficult or even impossible

### 1) Paused Listening tasks


- **Paused listening tasks: **An effective technique that shows the presence of relevant schematic knowledge facilitates comprehension.

- **Process-oriented listening****: **Enhances metacognitive awareness, Ss develop effective skills for self-regulating and listening comprehension.

- Pausing can be interpreted as a kind of scaffolding that enhances Ss' matacognitive awareness.

- T divides recordings into several segments and plays the segments at fairly regular intervals of pauses. During each pause, Ss write down what they believe the topic of the entire recording is, and which word or phrases make them think so.

- Clues activate competing content schemata

- Subin goes through changes in her understanding

## Input


### 1) Roughly-tuned input/ Finely-tuned input


- **Roughly-tuned input**: Input that is only accustomed at approximate level of Ss' proficiency. This is closer to natural usage of lg.

- **Finely-tuned input:** Input that is more carefully controlled to only include the structures and vocabulary that Ss have covered throughout the lessons. This presents clear examples of target grammar.

### 2) Lower speed of speech/ concocted texts


- Unnaturally lower speed of speech damages text authenticity.

- Putting pauses between phrases and sentences is better

- Concocted texts give false expectation about real-life listening experiences; Problems occur when Ss transfer what they learned in the classroom to the genuine experience outside the classroom.

- Authentic text exposes Ss to naturalness of lg; real-life listening experience

### 3) Strategies for using authentic materials for listening


- **Simplifying the tasks: **Instead of simplifying the lg presented in authentic material, Ts should simplify the tasks that are demanded of Ls. Ss can develop self-confidence and become highly motivated.

- **Grading the texts: **T can collect a large enough sample of authentic speeches and sequence them from the text which is more accessible to lower-level Ls to what is more appropriate to advanced Ls.

- **Staging the listening:** This provides graduated activities which require Ls to build up an understanding of the recording little by little. T first provides extremely simpler tasks, and then progress to ones that demand a closer understanding.

## Functions and Process of Listening


### 1) Transactional language function


- A function of spoken texts whose objective is to convey propositional or factual information; message-oriented; confirmation checks are used

- **Factual function**: giving and sending information
**Transactional function**: giving and obtaining goods and services.

- Exercises involve conveying information

### 2) Interactional language function


- A function of spoken texts whose objectives is to promote social relationship between interlocutors; person-oriented

- Exercises involve establishment and maintenance of social relationships

- Phatic communion (e.g. Nice day, isn't it?)

### 2) Bottom-up Processing


- Listening is a process of decoding the sounds that one hears in a linear fashion

- Moving from the parts to the whole

- Exercises that involve bottom-up listening: a)the focus in on form and the exercise deals with the structural system of English b) indicate selection of specific discrete items from the listening text, such as listening for details.

### 3) Top-down Processing


- Ls actively reconstruct the original meaning of the speaker using incoming sounds as clues.

- In reconstruction process, Ls use prior knowledge of the context and situation.

- Exercises that involve top-down listening: a)the focus is on meaning and the listener uses global listening strategies b) indicates a reliance on extralinguistic skills

### 4) Interactive Processing


- Top-down and bottom-up processing are simultaneously used

- It can cater to individual learning styles

- Appropriate for level-differentiated lesson as beginners can focus on bottom-up processing and advanced Ls can focus on top-down processing.

## Types Listening Practice and Strategies


### 1) Types of listening practice


- **Reactive Listening**: Listening technique where Ss simply listen to the surface structure of an utterance to repeat it back to the T.

- **Intensive Listening**
Listening to a text repeatedly to decode the input for lg analysis or practice in specific listening strategies.

- Ss focus on discrete points of the lg and to develop listening skills.

- It involves replaying a tape several times.

- **Responsive Listening**: Listening technique where Ss listen to the T's short utterance and make appropriate response to them. T 'pushes' output from Ls.

- **Selective Listening**
Listening with a planned purpose in mind in order to gather specific information that has been signaled prior to listening.

- Ss with field independent suit the task.

- Similar with scanning.

- Different from intensive listening as the discourse is longer.

- **Interactive Listening**: Listening technique integrated with speaking skills in authentic give and take of communicative interchange

Conversational interaction where Ls take a leading role in understanding

- **Extensive listening:** Listening to a longer stretch of speech for several minutes at a time, usually with a long-term goal of appreciating the content of the speech; Ss develop top-down skills.

윤도형: Extensive listening skills are used when Ss listen to unfamiliar speakers or to a variety of speakers.

### 2) Listening Strategies


- **Gist listening: **To understand only a topic

- **Scanning**: To figure out one or two pieces of specific information in a discourse without understanding every word

- **Listening for detail**: To find out several pieces of detailed information after intensively listening to a text

- **Inferencing**: Listening between the lines when information is incomplete; Ss go beyond the factual information, and analyze, interpret, and evaluate the meaning of a discourse.

윤도형: Ss can infer based on the text or on their general world knowledge.

- **Predicting:** To talk about the content that Ss are about to hear; this activates Ss' schemata and facilitates their comprehension

- **Monitoring**: To notice what Ss do and do not understand and confirm predictions; This happens while-listening

- Ss are asked to determine the accuracy of their prediction

- **Responding**: Ls provide a personal, relevant response to the information presented.

- **Clarifying**: To ask Qs and give feedback to the speaker in order to enhance the understanding. (What does _______ mean? You mean ________?)

### Teaching Speaking


## Speaking Activities


### 1) Drill


- **Drills: **A controlled technique that focuses on a minimal number of lg forms through some type of repetition and attempts to elicit responses from Ss

- **Mechanical Drills: **L is required to make the only one correct response from the T's cue; comprehension is not needed; no connection with reality

Ex) Repetition drills, substitution drills, moving slot substitution drills

- **Meaningful Drills: **A type of drill that has a predicted response with regulated sequence; some connection with reality; comprehension is needed to answer correctly

- **Communicative Practice: **A type of drill where the type of response is controlled but the L is given opportunities to provide an open response, to exchange real information, and to negotiate for meaning; referential Qs; require Ss to answer with personalized and real information

### 2) Discussion


- **Discussion: **Ss are divided into pairs or groups to discuss a certain topic

- Ss need to learn functional lg.

- Each S in a group should assume a specific role

- Skill-integrated activities

### 3) Presentation

### 5) Gap activities

- **Reasoning-gap activities**: An activity that requires Ss to derive new information by inferring it from the given information

### Teaching Writing


## Approaches in Teaching Writing


### 1) Product-oriented Approach


- **Product-oriented approach** focuses on producing an error-free final product with little concern for the processes involved in producing the end product

- Imitating, copying, transforming models of correct lg

- Quality matters more than quantity

- Emphasize Ss' final writing products

- Focus mostly on grammar and mechanics when giving feedback

### 2) Process-oriented Approach


- **Process-oriented approach** focuses on the steps Ss go through when drafting and redrafting their pieces of work, rather than a final product.

- Learner-centered

- Involves high-order processing such as idea development.

- Does NOT downplays the importance of the final product; however, it prefers well-organized and effect text over error free texts.

- Involve Ss in the process of planning, drafting, revising, and editing

- Focus mostly on content and organization when giving feedback

## Steps of Process-oriented approach


Writing process involve five basic stages: pre-writing; drafting; responding; revising; editing. As it is a recursive and cyclical process, any of these stages can be performed or re-performed at any stage in the writing process.

### 1) Pre-writing


Generating ideas

- **Brainstorming: **Ss spew out their ideas or associations about a given topic as many as possible, without being judged. It can be either done individually or in pair activity.

- **Clustering****:** An activity where Ss are asked to jot down all free associations triggered by the given key word to make a visual network linking them.

- **Mind map**: An activity where Ss scribble down ideas about a central topic and develop ideas as the mind makes associations.

- **Freewriting**: An activity where Ss freely write down about a given topic without stop, without formal correctness. T generally provides an opening clause or sentence for the Ss to start with.

- **Looping**: An activity where Ss write a short passage freely and rapidly, read their passage and select a sentence which conveys the most important idea from it, and use the sentence as the topic of other pieces of writing.

- **Strategic questioning****: **An activity where Ss generate who, why, what, where, when, and how Qs about a topic before writing.

Linguistic development

- Reading a model composition

### 2) Drafting (First Draft)


- Focus on fluency and delivery of meaning

- Conscious of context of writing (audience, expectation, purpose)

### 3-1) Responding from T


- **Formative feedback**: Feedback that aims at encouraging the development of their writing by helping them to see what they need to do next.

- **ECC(Essay Correction Code) or Correction Symbols**
+) Selectively points out the inaccurate lg issues; Elicit self-correction; Less effort; Less threatening for Ss

-) Useless when Ss fail to decode what symbols mean; Cannot give feedback on issues about content or organization; Cannot give information about how and why Ss made errors

- **Written feedback**

+) Clarifies existing ambiguities; Less threatening

-) Time-consuming; Passively incorporate T's suggestion

- Meet with individual Ss to help with work in progress

- This support revision process

- **Reformulation: **Providing feedback to Ss' 1st draft which requires Ss to compare a text written by one of them and its reformulated version made by the T

Reformulation helps Ss to notice the gap

### 3-2) Responding from Peer Ss


- Peer feedback, peer responding, peer editing, peer review

- Peer feedback focuses on content; peer editing focuses on forms; peer correction involves actual correction of errors

- +) Authentic context; authentic audience; more motivation; ability to critically analyze writings

- -) Vague, unhelpful; Too much emphasis on sentence-level problems; too much positive feedback

### 4) Revising


- **Revising:** Based on the feedback from responding stage, rewrite their draft in order to improve global issues like content and organization of ideas.

### 5) Editing


- **Editing**: Right before submitting their final draft, Ss check and improve local lg issues like grammar, spelling, punctuation, diction, and so on...

## Approaches


### 1) Focus on form


- **Focus on form**: An approach that deliberately draws Ss' attention to linguistic forms within the context of meaning; provides connection between communicative function and lg form

- **P****lanned focus on form**: There is a predetermined decision made by a teacher about specific target lg items embedded in meaning-focused activities; it has an intensive focus
**I****ncidental focus on form**: The attention to a particular linguistic form is drawn spontaneously, without prior intention, ranging from reactive, teacher-initiated feedback during meaning-focused activities to pre-emptive comments made in anticipation of S error; it has an extensive focus.

- **Reactive focus on form**: The T's corrective feedback to Ls' erroneous utterance while they are engaged in meaning-focused, communicative activities.
**Pre-emptive focus on form**: Form-focused instruction taken out from a communicative activity, by either the T or the S, to deal with a lg form which is expected to be problematic in advance even though no actual error has taken place.

- **Explicit focus on form**: FonF instruction that is provided in explicit grammar instruction which emphasizes acquisition of declarative knowledge
ex) CR task
**Implicit focus on form**: FonF instruction that is provided in implicit grammar instruction which emphasizes acquisition of procedural knowledge
ex) input enhancement, input flooding

- Techniques used in FonF are as follows: implicit negative feedback, input enhancement, input flooding, input processing instruction, structured-input activities, discovery-oriented tasks

- indirect approaches to grammar instruction

- map form onto function

- help develop language use through meaningful conversation

- discourse level analysis / context-embedded

- communicative use

- explain the target form in a contextualized setting

- combine formal instruction on the target forms and their communicative use

- raise Ss' awareness of the target forms through meaning-focused input

### 2) Focus on forms


- **Focus on forms**: An approach that explicitly teaches pre-selected lg items through explicit instructional methods where Ls' attention is overtly focused on form rather than meaning.

- **S****ynthetic syllabus**

- Lg consisted of a series of grammatical forms can be acquired sequentially.

- context-reduced, sentence level analysis

- traditional formal instruction where Ss are presented with grammar rules and practice exercises

- communicative usage

- explain the target form in a decontextualized setting

### 3) Focus on meaning


- **Focus on meaning**: An approach that immerses Ls in natural real-life communication, while assuming that learning a target lg incidentally and implicitly from comprehensible target lg samples is sufficient for successful language acquisition.

- **A****nalytic syllabus**

- Emphasizes pure meaning-based activity with no attention to form.

### 4) Inductive approach


- **Inductive approach**: Ss are presented with L2 data and asked to derive a rule from it.

- Rule discovery learning/ Discovery activity

- Data-driven learning

- Direct method, Natural approach

## Learning


- **Explicit learning**: A type of L2 learning with conscious awareness to the forms of lg; it consists of inductive explicit learning and deductive explicit learning.

**Implicit learning**: A type of L2 learning by means of unconscious exposure to input (e.g. input flooding)

- **Explicit grammar learning**: This is a type of learning that occurs when Ss are aware that they are learning grammar and target grammar rules which are clearly defined.

**Implicit grammar learning**: This is a type of learning that is based on the belief that grammar is naturally acquired; thus, grammar is learnt without intention or awareness.

- **Inductive explicit learning**: A type of explicit L2 learning where Ls are asked to discover an explicit rule from an array of examples provided; a 'rule-discovery' learning

**Deductive explicit learning**: A type of explicit L2 learning where Ls are given an explicit rule and then apply the rule to particular examples in practice activities; a 'rule-driven' learning

- **Intentional learning**: A type of L2 learning through a deliberate program of study to enhance the knowledge of target forms; this type of learning has clear purpose and draw Ls' attention to the target forms.

**Incidental learning**: a) A type of L2 learning in which either a L learns smt without the intention to learn it or b) L learns one thing while intending to learn another.

## Instruction


- **Intensive grammar instruction**: A type of grammar instruction where the Ls are taught specific target forms predetermined by the T. The forms are taught in repeated practice.

Ex) CR activities, GTM

- **Extensive grammar instruction**: A type of grammar instruction where specific forms are dealt in the form or corrective feedback during Ls' communicative interactions.

Ex) Language focus phase in TBI

- **Explicit grammar instruction**: A type of grammar instruction that involves problem-solving and reasoning. This intends Ss to develop declarative knowledge; explicit learning

Ex) GTM, CR-task/activities, garden path

- Promotes immediate gain of explicit knowledge of grammatical features

- **Implicit grammar instruction**: A type of grammar instruction that involves intellectually less demanding process. This intends Ss to develop procedural knowledge; explicit learning

Ex) ALM

- Promotes immediate gain of implicit knowledge of grammatical features

### 3) Input Processing Instruction


- **Input processing instruction**: A type of grammar instruction that is designed to help Ss to overcome their excessive focus on meaning over forms when processing input.

- **Input processing theory**: A theory that assumes that Ls have preference for semantic processing over morphological processing, so that they process input for meaning before they process it for form

A corpus is searched and analyzed by a concordancing program or concordancer. The result of a corpus search is displayed either alphabetically or on the basis of frequency. This display is known as a concordance.

# Ch.13 Assessment

### Assessment


## Qualities of Language Assessment


### 1) Practicality


- **Practicality**: The degree to which a test will be implemented within the means of a) financial limitations, b) time constraints, c) ease of administration

### 2) Reliability


- **Reliability:** The degree to which a test gives consistent and dependable results.

- **Internal-consistency reliability****: **The degree to which all test items or tasks measure the same thing; it is related to homogeneity of the test items

- **Student reliability****: **The degree to which every test-taker has the consistent conditions

- **Test administration reliability****: **The degree to which the environment where a test is performed is consistent

- **Intra-rater reliability****: **The degree to which the results of scoring performed by a single scorer are consistent

- **Inter-rater reliability****: **The degree to which the results of scoring by two or more scorers are consistent

- **Test-retest reliability****: **Correlation between the first test administration and the second test administration both of which share the same test forms and the same test-taker samples but have some time gaps

- **Equivalent-forms reliability (Parallel-forms reliability)****: **The consistency of two different versions of the test instrument which use different equipment but measure the same thing, and have similar level of difficulty, discrimination, and content coverage ** **

- T developed two tests where items in each set had similar levels of difficulty, discrimination, and content coverage.

- **Split-half reliability: **The consistency of two randomly divided sections of a single test.

### 3) Validity


- **Validity**: The degree to which a test exactly and faithfully measures what it is supposed to measure

- **Content validity:**** **The degree to which the content of the assessment covers, samples the content and objectives of the course being assessed. It is determined by a) item representativeness, and b) domain coverage

- The followings have low content-validity: a) Standard proficiency test; b) Paper-and-pencil multiple-choice test to assess Ss' speaking competence; c) Cloze test in the speaking course

- 측정하고자 하는 skill 이외에 아예 다른 skill을 측정하면 content validity가 낮아짐

- T include too many Qs about material that she had not dealt with in class

- **Face validity****: **The degree to which test-takers feel as if a test is measuring what it is supposed to measure.

- We are middle school Ss, but the exam 'looked like' one that high school Ss normally take: the printing was too small, and we had to read five pages in one hour. I really hated it

- **Construct validity****: **The degree to which a given assessment instrument taps into the theoretical construct as it has been defined.

- **Construct: **An ability that T intend to measure by means of the test instrument; it consists of multiple underlying component traits

- If the content validity is low, construct validity should be ensured. (e.g. standardized proficiency test) ** **

- The following examples have low construct validity: a) Oral interview test where the score is determined by pronunciation and grammar; b) Word definition quiz used in the course whose objective is the communicative use of vocabulary

- 측정하고자 하는 skill의 sub-skill을 측정하지 않을 경우 construct validity가 낮아짐

- Most of the Qs were dictation Qs. We even had to write a full paragraph for one Q. It seemed like writing test rather than a listening test

- Related to the meaningfulness and appropriateness of the interpretations that we make on the basis of test scores

- Construct validity needs to be justified in order to interpret scores from lg tests as indicators of the test taker's lg ability

- **Concurrent validity****: **The degree to which results of a test correlate with those of another test which is performed at about the same time and aimed at measuring the same skill

- **Predictive validity: **The degree to which a test accurately predicts future performances

### 4) Authenticity


- **Authenticity****: **The degree of correspondence of the characteristics of a given lg test task to the features of a target lg use task in the generalized context outside the test-taking situation.

- Lg should be natural; items should be contextualized; thematic organization to items should be provided; topics are meaningful

- Related to the conventional concept of content validity

- Domain of generalization: Performance on lg tests corresponds to lg use in specific domains other than the lg test itself

### 5) Interactiveness


- **Interactiveness****:** The degree of involvement of the test-taker's individual characteristics in accomplishing a test task

- Ls' individualistic characters relevant to lg testing are as follows: lg ability, topical knowledge, affective schemata

- Authenticity & Interactiveness

- High authenticity & Low interactiveness: Typing test task for applicants for typists

- High authenticity & High interactiveness: Role play where Ss play shop keeper and customer

- Low authenticity & Low interactiveness: Vocabulary test used in entrance exams for American universities

- Low authenticity & High interactiveness: Interviewing a typist about favorite food and sports team.

### 6) Washback


- **Washback**** effect**: The effect of testing on teaching and learning

- a) Information delivered to Ls in the form of useful diagnoses of their strengths and weakness, b) Effect of testing on instruction in terms of how Ss prepare for the test.

- Informal assessment has more built-in washback effects as T provides interactive feedback

- Formal assessment deliver no washback effect if the grade is delivered by a simple letter grade

- Comment generously and specifically, be open to discuss the feedback and evaluation that you have given

## Types of testing


### 0) Formal, Informal testing


- **Formal assessment**: Systematic exercises designed to judge Ss' achievement. (e.g. formal testing)

- **Informal assessment**: Incidental comments during the class designed to a) elicit Ss' lg performance, b) give them diagnostic feedback, but c) not to judge their achievements

### 1) Formative, Summative test/ assessment


- **Formative test/ assessment**: a type of assessment which evaluates Ss during the process of instruction in order to scaffold and promote their learning process

- Has beneficial washback effects

- Portfolio assessment is formal and formative assessment

- Guides the subsequent teaching and learning process

- **Summative test/ assessment**: type of assessment which measures what Ss have learned and how well they accomplished the instructional objectives at the end of the course in order to document their learning outcomes.

- Rarely provides washback effects

- All summative assessment is formal assessment

- Diagnose Ss' learning needs for the future course

- Summative feedback: A total score for the reading section

### 2) Norm-referenced, Criterion-referenced testing


- **Norm-referenced testing: **a type of testing under which Ls' scores can be compared with each other

- The primary purpose is to place test takers along a mathematical continuum in rank order.

- Practicality, reliability, validity are primary concerns

- Test results are reported in the form of a numerical score or a percentile rank

- Shows the relative standing of each test-taker in terms of performance

- Adopts framework of comparison btwn individuals for understanding the significance of any score

- Distinctions btwn individuals are made

- Reliability and validity are well established

- Inappropriately competitive learning climate and discourage for the average Ss

- **Criterion-referenced testing****:** a type of testing which evaluates Ls' performances against an absolute level of mastery at a given level.

- Gives qualitative information (eg. feedback) about what test-takers can do with the lg  to attain the learning goal

- The primary purpose is to give useful feedback on Ss' progress in relation to learning goals

- Authenticity and washback are primary concerns

- Individual performances are evaluated against a verbal description of a satisfactory performance at a given level

- A series of performance goals can be set for individual Ls

- Motivation is maintained and the striving is for a personal best rather than against other LS

### 3) Discrete-point, Integrative assessment


- **Discrete-point assessment****: **a test which uses a series of separated test tasks to assess one bit or piece of language at a time

- Based on the assumption that language can be broken down into its component parts and those parts can be separately tested

- This test assumes that the language proficiency is the mastery of separate points of linguistic system such as phonological, lexical, and grammatical system.

- It can assess linguistic knowledge AND sociocultural knowledge

- +) Reliable and practical

- -) a) Provide little information on Ss' ability to use the lg and its function in real-world context; b) communicative competence cannot be assessed with this method; c) systematic relationship of lg is ignored

- **Integrative assessment**: a type of assessment which assesses more than one thing at a time

- Requires Ss to use multiple aspects of lg ability

- Accuracy of linguistic detail can be ignored

- Dictation, cloze test, oral interview

### 4) Direct, Indirect assessment


- **Direct assessment**: a method of testing which requires Ss to perform the target skill

- **Indirect assessment****:** a method of testing attempts to measure Ss' ability that underlies the target skill.

### 5) Classroom Language Testing


- **Proficiency test****: **a test designed to give information about Ss' overall lg proficiency regardless of any instruction they may have had previously, so that the upcoming course can be pitched at the appropriate level of difficulty

- **Placement test****: **a test designed to sort Ls into groups of the similar proficiency level

- **Selection test: **A test designed to ascertain whether a person has sufficient lg proficiency to begin a particular course of study

- **Diagnostic test****: **a test designed to determine which features are difficult for Ss and include those parts in the curriculum by identifying Ls' strengths and weaknesses, as well as their needs

- **Progress Achievement test****: **a test designed to give diagnostic feedback or formative information about how much Ss have learned and how well they are learning

- **Final Achievement test****:**** **a test designed to give summative information about how much Ss have learned in a particular unit of instruction, how well they acquired what they are expected to learn, and how successfully they attained the course objectives at the end of the course

## Scoring methods of Productive skills


### 1) Holistic Scoring Method


- **Holistic Scoring Method**: a scoring method used to measure the productive skills which use a variety of criteria to offer a single score for a task completion without providing specific information about subskills involved in the task completion

- Based on the overall impressionistic assessment of the Ss' performance on writing or speaking tasks

- **Primary Trait Scoring**: A scoring method that focuses on how well Ss can write within a narrowly defined range of discourse. (e.g. if an essay is ought to persuade the reader, the assessment is made whether the function has been achieved or not)

- **Holistic Rubric**: the type of scoring rubric for Ss' writing which describes in general terms the qualities of excellent, good, fair, and unsatisfactory assignments in forms of a single letter or numerical score followed by written comment.

- Advantages

- Rapid scoring is possible

- Show overall lg proficiency, and achieve high (construct) validity

- Disadvantages

- Cannot provide useful diagnostic information about Ls' strengths or weakness

### 2) Analytic Scoring Method


- **Analytic Scoring Method: **a scoring method used to measure the productive skills which separately rates the various components involved in a lg performance and provides scores for each component without giving a single entire grade

- Different weights are given to separate components to reflect their importance in lg instruction

- **Weighted rubric: **the type of scoring rubric for Ss' writing which breaks the writing skills into categories and sub-categories and assigns a specific point value to each.

- Advantages

- Ss are given useful feedback

- Useful to pinpoint the strength

- Provides formative feedback

- Disadvantages

- Takes more time

- Overall lg proficiency cannot be seen

- **Rational deletion:** a cloze test in which the deleted words are determined according to the grammatical or discourse functions of the words

- **Exact word scoring method:** a scoring method of cloze test by which Ss get credits for correct answers if and only if they fill in the blanks with the exact words deleted from the original text; this method bears high practicality and high reliability

- **Appropriate (Acceptable) word scoring method**: a scoring method of cloze test by which a response that is grammatically correct and makes good sense in the context is given a full credit; this method bears high face validity.

- C-test

- **C-test:** Adapted version of the cloze test where the second half of every other words is delted, leaving the first and the last sentence of the passage intact

- High reliability and validity

- But Ss can fill in the blanks by relying on micro-level processing rather than high-order processing

### 2) Assessing Listening


- **Listening cloze**: a listening task in which Ss listen to a story while simultaneously reading and completing its written transcript in which selected words and phrases have been deleted.

- Requires selective listening

- Should adopt rational deletion type and the exact word scoring method

- Should delete the words with high information load

- **Information transfer:** a technique which requires Ss to reinterpret and transform the information from one mode by which they receive the input to another mode

- Scanning strategies and selective listening are promoted

- Minimal demands on production skills

- Greater authenticity

- Encourages deep processing of input as Ss transform the input in some way

### 3) Assessing Speaking


- **Role play: **a drama technique where Ss take the roles of different persons and act them out in a relatively short and brief situation which is either realistic or a pure fantasy

- Elicits active S participation

- Realistic rehearsal for real-world experiences

- Less cognitive load

- Development of pragmatic competence

- Motivation

- Performance beyond current competence

## Multiple-choice Test Tasks


### 1) Guidelines for Making Multiple-choice Test Tasks


- For stem

- Stem should be concise and simple

- Every stem on the test should assess part of the construct of interest

- Must not contain inadvertent clues

- Avoid making the stem identical to material that was used in class

- Should be presented in context

- A good context is very important for the structure chosen in a sentence

- Should avoid a stem that provides an unintended clue to the right answer if they want to make score interpretation valid

- Test taker should refer to the context to answer the Qs.

- The problems of all choices being correct answers can be easily solved by providing more meaningful context through the task .

- For response alternatives

- Correct answers should be genuinely correct

- Distractors should belong to the same grammatical and semantic category as the correct response

- All response alternatives should be grammatically compatible with the stem

- Must avoid more than one correct alternatives

- All distractors should be equally attractive to test-takers

- All response alternatives should be made equally in terms of difficulty, complexity, and length

- Correct responses should not be derived from common knowledge (world knowledge)

- Alternative responses should be as simple as possible

- Item independence should be observed
